Phenomenon — first, we just look

Fred, Heine — close your eyes. Imagine a tree in Kenya.

It is slim and not very tall — maybe as tall as your father. Its leaves are small and feathery, like tiny green coins arranged in rows along a stalk. When you rub a leaf between your fingers, it smells a little like horseradish or a radish from the market in Frankfurt. If you snap a young branch, the wood is soft and pale, not hard like oak. The seeds inside the pods look like small nuts with three paper wings. This is Moringa oleifera.

Fred asks:
"But how do we know what it really is? Maybe it's just a weed?"
Your minister answers:
Good question. That is exactly the right first question. Detection comes before knowledge. We look. We describe. We separate what we see from what we already believe.
Detection — naming what we notice

What our five senses tell us

  • Sight: Feathery, pinnate leaves. Long seed pods, like a green drumstick. Pale, cream-white flowers.
  • Touch: Leaves are soft. Seeds are smooth with papery wings. Trunk bark is pale grey and corky.
  • Smell: Leaves smell sharp, peppery. Roots smell like horseradish.
  • Taste: Leaves are slightly bitter and peppery. Young pods taste like green beans.
  • Sound: In the wind, the feathery leaves make a soft rustling, like a whisper.
Description — the formal record

Its name and family

Scientists call it Moringa oleifera Lam. — the word oleifera means "oil-bearing" in Latin. It belongs to the family Moringaceae. Its common names include the Drumstick Tree, the Horseradish Tree, and in Swahili, Mlonge. In Kenya, where our Kavutiri Farm sits, it grows well in the red soils of Kirinyaga County.

Kingdom: Plantae Order: Brassicales Family: Moringaceae Genus: Moringa Species: oleifera
Known states — things we can say with confidence

What the scientists have shown us

  • Moringa leaves contain more protein per gram than many meats — including compounds called amino acids that our bodies use to build muscles.
  • The leaves contain beta-carotene, which our body turns into Vitamin A — good for eyes and skin.
  • Scientists have found calcium, potassium, iron, and zinc in the leaves — minerals our bones and blood need.
  • Laboratory studies show strong antioxidant properties — meaning the plant contains chemicals that help protect our body's cells.
  • The tree grows in dry conditions. It survives drought. This makes it important for Africa.

Unknown states — things we are still learning

What we do not yet fully know

  • The exact dose for children has not been established in large clinical trials.
  • Some compounds in the root and bark may be harmful in large amounts — which is why we only use the leaves.
  • Whether moringa grown in Hesse soil would have the same properties as moringa grown in Kirinyaga is not fully tested.
  • Long-term effects of daily moringa use across a lifetime — not fully documented.
  • Interaction with some medicines — being studied.
Fred, Heine — notice this: science does not hide what it doesn't know. Admitting the unknown is not weakness. It is the beginning of discovery.

The Anglican rhetorical tradition

Scripture, Reason, Tradition — the three-legged stool

Heine asks:
"Why is a minister talking about a plant? Is that not for scientists?"
Your minister answers:
Richard Hooker, a great Anglican thinker from the 1500s, taught us that God gave us three gifts for understanding truth: the words of Scripture, the voice of human Reason, and the wisdom of Tradition — of what people have learned and passed down. Moringa has been used by communities in Africa and Asia for thousands of years. That is tradition. Scientists have now tested it. That is reason. And caring for our bodies, especially the bodies of children, is a responsibility Scripture gives us. All three legs of the stool stand together.
Gestalt principles — seeing the whole, not just the parts

Why we look at the whole tree, not just one leaf

The psychologists who study how we see things discovered something important: our minds want to see the whole picture, not just the pieces. This is called Gestalt — a German word meaning "whole form" or "shape." For Moringa, this means:

  • The whole plant matters: leaves, seeds, pods, roots, bark — all have different properties.
  • The whole person matters: nutrition, culture, community, faith — not only the chemical compound.
  • The whole community matters: one child taking moringa tells us little; many children over many years tells us much.
  • Context matters: moringa in Kirinyaga soil, in Kirinyaga sun, may be different from moringa in a bottle.
What moringa does for you

The evidence from peer-reviewed science

  • Nutrition: Dried leaves contain 27% protein — more than eggs. They carry all essential amino acids. (Fahey, 2005; Anwar et al., 2007)
  • Vitamins: Rich in Vitamin A, Vitamin C, Vitamin E, and B-vitamins including folate. (Coppin, 2013; Leone et al., 2015)
  • Minerals: Contains calcium comparable to milk, more potassium than bananas, iron, zinc, magnesium. (Pakade et al., 2013; Yaméogo et al.)
  • Antioxidants: High levels of polyphenols and flavonoids that protect cells. (Sreelatha & Padma, 2009)
  • Anti-inflammatory: Studies suggest compounds that reduce inflammation in the body. (Gopalakrishnan et al., 2016)
  • Blood sugar: Early research shows promise for supporting healthy glucose levels. (Mbikay, 2012)
  • Skin: Seed oil used in creams and sunscreens for its nourishing and protective properties. (Yanuarti et al.; Gyawali et al.)
Developmental strategy — why this matters for you, right now

Fred, Heine — you are 10. Your body is building itself.

Between age 8 and 14, your brain forms its fastest new connections since you were a baby. Your bones are laying down calcium that you will use for the rest of your life. Your immune system is learning to recognise the world. This window — right now — is when good nutrition leaves the deepest mark.

A small amount of moringa powder, added to food you already enjoy, can quietly support this building work. It is not medicine. It is food — very dense, very old food, eaten by people in Kenya for centuries.

Your parents will know what to add to what you eat. Your minister is working with them. You are not experimental subjects — you are children being cared for, and you deserve to understand why.

Transmission — the story passed down

How moringa travelled through time

Ancient India, ~2000 BCE
Ayurvedic medicine begins using moringa to treat over 300 conditions. Ancient texts describe its leaves, seeds, and roots.
Ancient Egypt and Rome
Romans and Egyptians extract oil from moringa seeds for perfume, skin care, and cooking. It is traded across the ancient world.
East Africa — centuries of use
In Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, communities plant moringa near homes. Mothers feed the leaves to nursing babies. Elders use the roots for flavour and medicine.
1998–2005 — scientific surge
International researchers begin systematic studies. Fahey's landmark 2005 review gathers all available evidence. The plant enters global nutritional science.
2007–2016 — peer review era
Anwar, Leone, Gopalakrishnan, Mbikay and others publish detailed nutritional and pharmacological analyses. The evidence base grows solid.
Today — Kavutiri Farm, Kirinyaga
On our Gibbs Coffee farm in Kirinyaga County, moringa grows alongside coffee. Its leaves feed people. Its story continues. You are part of it now.
~2056 — your turn
